What is Diabetic Retinopathy(DR)?

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a complication of diabetes that damages the light-sensitive retina in your eye. High blood sugar weakens tiny blood vessels in the retina, causing them to leak fluid or bleed. In some cases, abnormal new blood vessels grow, further threatening your vision. Early DR often has no symptoms, but regular eye exams can detect it before vision loss occurs.

Various Stages in Diabetic Retinopathy

1. No Diabetic Retinopathy:

* In this early stage, there are no visible signs of retinopathy in the eye. The retinal blood vessels appear normal, and there are no abnormalities detected during an eye examination.

2. Mild Diabetic Retinopathy :

* This stage is characterized by the development of microaneurysms, small bulges in the retinal blood vessels caused by weakened vessel walls. There may also be tiny areas of retinal swelling or leakage, known as retinal hemorrhages or exudates.

3. Moderate Diabetic Retinopathy :

* In this stage, the number of microaneurysms and other abnormalities increases. Cotton wool spots (fluffy white lesions) may appear, indicating areas of poor retinal circulation. Venous beading (irregular constrictions of the retinal veins) may also be present.

4. Severe Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):

* This stage is characterized by a significant number of retinal hemorrhages, microaneurysms, and other abnormalities. There may be areas of retinal ischemia (lack of blood supply) due to the closure of many retinal blood vessels. Vision may be affected, but there is no growth of new abnormal blood vessels yet.

5. Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):

* This is the most advanced stage of diabetic retinopathy. In response to the lack of oxygen supply, new abnormal blood vessels (neovascularization) begin to grow from the retina or optic nerve. These fragile new vessels can bleed into the vitreous humor, causing vision loss or blindness. Scar tissue may also develop, leading to retinal detachment or the formation of glaucoma.
